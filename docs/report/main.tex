\subsection{Implementation Details and Reproducibility}

In order to guarantee complete reproducibiblity, the seed for all random functions used during training was set to 42.
All train functions followed the same structure:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Setup of models, directories and variables;
    \item Main training loop with 50 epochs, with checkpoints and samples every 10 epochs.
    \item Saving of the final model
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Latent Denoise Network}

The Latent Denoise Network model's architecture was the same as the one in the PL classes.
For the diffusion process, the DDPM scheduler used was the GaussianDiffusion model.
For the time steps embeddings, a Sinusoidal Position Embedding, with another 3 layers of Linear, SiLu and Linear transformations, respectivelly, were used.
For the reverse diffusion step, ResnetBlocks in sequence were used.
For the encoding and decoding of samples to and from the latent space, the VAE previously trained was used.

The only changes made to the provided code were to the number of latent channels, which had to be the same as the trained VAE latent channels', 
and the implementation of the sample, train step and image generation functions inside the class of the model.
These sample and train step functions were implemented just like the respective PL class demanded.

\subsubsection{Pixel UNet}

The Pixel UNet model's architecture, just like the previous model, was the same as the one in the Diffusion models's PL class.
The diffusion process and the time steps embeddings were the same as the ones in the previous model.
The only differences were in the reverse diffusion step, where 6 ResnetBlocks were used (2 in downscaling, 2 keeping the resolution and 2 in upscaling), 
and the lack of a latent space.

Similarly to the Latent Denoise Network, the main changes to the code were with the implementation of the sample, train step and image generation inside the class of the model.
These functions are very similar to the ones implemented with the previous model, only with slight adaptations.
The only other difference from the PL class's code was the change in the input channels, which became 3, due to the dataset images being represented in the RGB space.

\section{Experimental Design}
\subsection{Hyperparameter Optimization Strategy}
We performed hyperparameter optimization with Bayesian optimization (Optuna). To keep the search feasible on standard hardware, 
tuning was conducted on a development subset comprising 20\% of the training data. For each model family, candidate configurations 
were trained on a training split and selected using performance on a held-out validation split. We minimized the following 
surrogate objectives: (i) \textit{VAE}: validation reconstruction loss; (ii) \textit{GAN}-based models: validation sum of discriminator 
and generator losses; (iii) \textit{Diffusion models}: validation denoising loss under a fixed Gaussian noise schedule.
The hyperparameter search spaces are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:hpo_spaces}, and the best configurations found on the studies are 
reported in Table~\ref{tab:hpo_best}. We used validation losses as surrogates during tuning because FID/KID are computationally expensive 
and high-variance at the per-trial scale: they require generating large sample sets and repeated feature extraction. In contrast, validation losses are 
available at each epoch and exhibit lower variance which improves sample efficiency under limited computational resources. We note, however, that GAN losses are far from beeing perfect proxies 
for perceptual sample quality; therefore, we use this objective only for hyperparameter selection during tuning and rely on qualitative inspection 
for the selected final parameters.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}
Across the selected configurations (Table~\ref{tab:hpo_best}), 
the optimization process consistently favored smaller batch sizes. 
This aligns with the well-documented generalization gap observed in 
large-batch training~\cite{keskar}. Specifically, the inherent noise 
in small-batch stochastic gradients serves as a form of implicit 
regularization, facilitating the escape from sharp local minima and 
leading the model toward flatter, more robust regions of the loss 
landscape. Regarding the learning rates, the observed values
are relatively low, consistent with the need for stable convergence and
stability during training, especially for GANs which are known to be sensitive to hyperparameter settings.
Latent dimensions reveal important differences across model families: the VAE favored a much smaller 
latent space (32) compared to the DCGAN (256), which may reflect the different in the 
model's architectures: GANs don't pay an explicity penalty (KL divergence)
for using larger latent spaces, while VAEs are encouraged to find compact representations.
A larger latent space may encourage more diverse samples as long as the generator
actually uses it. For the VAE, the optimal $\beta$ value is very low, 
indicating that the model prioritizes reconstruction fidelity over latent space regularization.
For the DCGAN, the optimal $\beta_1$ value is around 0.48, which is close 
to the commonly used value of 0.5~\cite{dcgan}, suggesting a balance between stability and convergence speed. 
The real\_label parameter being close to 1 (0.9836) indicates that the discriminator 
benefits from a small anti-overconfidence margin, which can help prevent it from becoming too confident 
and overpowering the generator during training without weakening the learning signal too much.

\section{Results and Interpretation}

\subsection{Quantitative Results}
Table~\ref{tab:summary_results} summarizes the quantitative evaluation across model families using 5{,}000 generated 
samples and 10 random seeds per model. We report mean $\pm$ standard deviation across seeds. 
Arrows indicate whether lower ($\downarrow$) or higher ($\uparrow$) values are preferred. 
The best value in each metric is highlighted in bold.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Summary of quantitative results (mean $\pm$ std across seeds).}
    \label{tab:summary_results}
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Model} & \textbf{FID $\downarrow$} & \textbf{KID $\downarrow$} & \textbf{Precision $\uparrow$} & \\
        \midrule
        VAE & 134.228 $\pm$ 0.531 & 0.13136 $\pm$ 0.00109 & 0.80548 $\pm$ 0.00671\\
        DCGAN & \textbf{101.248 $\pm$ 0.864} & \textbf{0.07664 $\pm$ 0.00153} & 0.86400 $\pm$ 0.00489 \\
        PixelUNet & 116.933 $\pm$ 0.708 & 0.09972 $\pm$ 0.00107 & \textbf{0.90804 $\pm$ 0.00512} \\
        LatentDenoiser & 119.271 $\pm$ 0.416 & 0.10068 $\pm$ 0.00122 & 0.78472 $\pm$ 0.00466 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Overall, DCGAN achieves the best performence in terms of 
global distributional alignment obtaining the lowest FID (101.248 $\pm$ 0.864)
and KID (0.07664 $\pm$ 0.00153) scores. This suggests that the DCGAN is more effective at 
capturing the overall data distribution compared to the other models. The VAE, on the 
other hand, has the worst performance in terms of FID and KID, 
which is consistent with the known limitations of VAEs in generating overly
smooth reconstructions due to the variational objective, which
can lead to blurry samples and less realistic outputs.
PixelUNet achieves the highest precision (0.90804 $\pm$ 0.00512), indicating 
that it generates a higher proportion of samples that are close to the 
real data manifold. However, the higher FID and KID values
compared to DCGAN suggest that the improvement in sample realism 
does not necessarily translate to better overall distributional alignment.
They, therefore, generate more realistic samples but with less diversity.
The LatentDenoiser achieves intermediate FID and KID values but the 
lowest precision among the evaluated models. This suggests that while 
its generated distribution is moderately aligned with the target dataset 
at a global level, a larger fraction of its individual samples fall outside 
the real data manifold compared to the other architectures. Such behavior may 
indicate incomplete convergence or reduced stability in the denoising process 
during sampling. In addition, the small standard deviations across seeds for all 
models suggest that the results are consistent and not heavily influenced by
random initialization or stochastic training dynamics which is a positive sign of the 
robustness of the training process.
